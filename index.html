Doomed to fall: how the ISS will be decommissioned

Abstract Main Article Presentation References

Abstract

The International Space Station (ISS) has been a staple of humanity’s great achievements for nearly 30 years, however it is also the most expensive at an estimated €100 billion (ESA, ND). Meant only to last 15 years it has been asked why at nearly $3 billion per year (Howell, 2025) a new station has not been built to replace it. Many plans for its decommissioning have been put forward, and in 2022 NASA finally decided on a course of action: a controlled re-entry. In simple terms, it means carefully pushing the ISS into a collision course with Earth via a booster rocket (Foust, 2024). A large amount of it will burn up harmlessly in the atmosphere, while larger parts will land in a predetermined location, most likely in the Pacific Ocean. This is not the end for human occupied space stations however, as multiple plans have been put forward for newer stations, with one station already in use (Horack, 2025).

Main Article

Beginnings and life in orbit

The ISS began life during the cold war collaboration between the USA and USSR. Both wanted a manned space station, and neither had the funds to complete it on their own. They decided to build a joint station, with the goal of multinational cooperation in space. In November 1998 the first module of the ISS was launched into Low Earth Orbit (LEO) (see Figure 2). 2 years later, the first humans went aboard the ISS. The European Space Agency, Japan and Canada also aided in expanding and repairing the station across its life (ISS National Laboratory, ND).

The station is invaluable to the scientific community due to its microgravity, allowing for scientific experiments not possible on Earth (Tat et al. 2023, p. 1). LEO allowed for shorter journeys between Earth and the station while also burning up micro meteorites and debris, helping protect the station, although larger debris has struck the station previously adding to repair costs. LEO however comes with a challenge - constant temperature change. The ISS’s external temperature can go from 200°C to -200°C, 16 times a day (NASA, 2007). This constant expansion and contraction reduces the lifespan of the station. Various upgrades and repairs of the station have been required, leading to the question as to why an aging station is being upgraded, when it could be easier to simply build a new one. As such, in 2022 NASA decided to decommission the ISS (King, 2022).

Plans for decommissioning

Decommissioning the ISS is not an easy task. At 110m long and 420 tonnes (NASA, 2007), many plans were put forward on how to decommission the ISS safely and cost effectively. The ISS could not be left in its current orbit, as without regularly boosting it would crash into Earth within months on a random re-entry. This would pose a major risk to both other satellites in orbit and the human population on Earth (NASA, 2024).

The idea of simply pushing the station into a further orbit was brought forward, with the goal of preserving the station for future use and as a potential museum, but this is unfeasible. It would take a huge amount of fuel using a vehicle akin to SpaceX’s Starship, the largest rocket ever launched, and would leave the station at the mercy of space. It would likely end up breaking apart, leaving debris in orbit and potentially jeopardising future projects: "NASA has estimated that such an impact could permanently degrade or even eliminate access to low Earth orbit for centuries". (NASA, 2024).

Returning the station to Earth piece by piece to be placed in a museum was also considered, however this was deemed too difficult and costly. The ISS was constructed with the help the Space Shuttles, "taking 13 years, 27 flights and 161 extravehicular activities". The last Space Shuttle flight was in 2011, and reactivating the programme would cost an unjustifiable amount of money (NASA, 2024). Instead, a controlled re-entry was chosen. The ISS will continue operations until 2030, when a Deorbit Vehicle built by SpaceX will push the ISS into a controlled re-entry (see Figure 3), with the likely landing point being Point Nemo (Foust, 2024). This is the most remote location in the ocean, with the nearest land being 2,600km away (see Figure 4). Other space debris has landed here before under a controlled re-entry, although nothing as large as the ISS (NOAA, 2021).

Legacy and the future of human presence in space

The idea of a manned space station is not over. In 2021 China started launching modules for its Tiangong space station, which it currently mans (Tat et al. 2023, p. 2). NASA has also looked at using private contractors to build a space station, and in 2020 awarded Axiom Space with a contract to build the Axiom space station as early as 2028 (see Figure 5). The ISS will be used to help build the Axiom before it is released to orbit on its own, thus helping next generation of space stations (NASA, 2024). As stated before, the ISS could not be brought back piece by piece to be into a museum, however it could partially be recovered. In 2013, billionaire Jeff Bezos led a team to recover the Saturn V engines used in the Apollo 11 mission from the Pacific Ocean. The recovered pieces are now on display in the Museum of Flight in Seattle (David, 2017). There is no reason not to believe that something similar could not be achieved with the parts the ISS that survive re-entry. A piece of history with such importance as the ISS should not be left at the bottom of the Ocean given its cultural and techonlogical impact on humanity.
